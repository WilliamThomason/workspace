PART 1 — Interview

Part 1: Interview

The examiner asks questions about everyday topics. Candidates answer individually.

Format

Duration: 2 minutes (pair) / 3 minutes (group of 3)

Focus: Giving personal information, expressing opinions on familiar topics

Topics covered:

  • Daily life and routines
  • Education and school
  • Hobbies and free time
  • Travel and holidays
  • Family and friends
  • Future plans and ambitions

Candidates should give full answers — not just "yes" or "no". Aim for 2-3 sentences per answer.

PART 1 — Practice
Timer: 2 minutes — Answer all questions

Interview Questions

Answer each question in turn. Speak in full sentences. The timer starts automatically.

  1. Where do you live, and what do you like about your area?
  2. What subjects are you studying at school at the moment?
  3. Do you prefer studying alone or with friends? Why?
  4. What do you usually do at the weekend?
  5. Have you been to any interesting places recently?
  6. What would you like to do after you finish school?
Examiner tip

Don't memorise answers. The examiner can tell. Speak naturally and show your personality.

PART 2 — Long Turn

Part 2: Long Turn

Each candidate speaks for 1 minute about two photographs. Then the other candidate answers a short question.

Candidate A

Looks at two photos for 1 minute

Compares them and answers a question

Speaks for 1 minute

Candidate B

Listens to Candidate A

Answers a short question about the photos

Speaks for 30 seconds

Key skill

Compare the photos — don't just describe them. Use language of similarity and difference: "Both photos show...", "In the first photo... whereas in the second..."

PART 2 — Practice A
Timer: 1 minute — Compare the photos

Long Turn: Set A

Candidate A: Look at these two photographs. They show people learning in different ways. Compare the photographs and say what you think the people are enjoying about learning in these different ways.

Photo A: Students in a science laboratory doing an experiment together

Photo B: A teenager sitting alone at a desk reading a textbook with a laptop

Follow-up (Candidate B)

Which way of learning do you prefer? (30 seconds)

PART 2 — Practice B
Timer: 1 minute — Compare the photos

Long Turn: Set B

Candidate B: Look at these two photographs. They show people helping the environment in different ways. Compare the photographs and say how effective you think these different ways of helping the environment are.

Photo A: A group of young people picking up litter on a beach

Photo B: A family installing solar panels on their roof

Follow-up (Candidate A)

Which activity would you prefer to do? (30 seconds)

PART 3 — Collaborative Task

Part 3: Collaborative Task

Candidates discuss a situation together and make a decision. The examiner gives a question with five prompts.

Format

Phase 1: 2 minutes — Discuss all five prompts together

Phase 2: 1 minute — Make a final decision together

Skills tested:

  • Exchanging ideas and opinions
  • Agreeing and disagreeing politely
  • Suggesting and speculating
  • Negotiating and reaching a decision
  • Justifying your choices

This is a discussion — talk TO each other, not to the examiner. Build on what your partner says.

PART 3 — Practice A
Timer: 2 minutes — Discuss all options

Collaborative Task A

A local youth centre wants to organise a weekend activity for teenagers. Here are some ideas and a question for you to discuss. First, talk to each other about how useful each activity would be for teenagers. Then decide which two activities the youth centre should organise.

Discuss these options
  • 🎵 A music festival with local bands
  • 🏀 A sports tournament with different games
  • 🎨 An art and crafts workshop
  • 🍳 A cooking class with a professional chef
  • 🎮 An e-sports gaming competition

Phase 2 (after timer): Decide together which TWO activities would be most popular.

PART 3 — Decision
Timer: 1 minute — Make your final decision

Make Your Decision

Now decide together: which two activities should the youth centre organise? Be ready to explain your reasons.

Useful language

"I think we should choose... because..."

"That's a good point, but I feel that..."

"How about we go with...?"

"I agree with you. Let's decide on..."

PART 3 — Practice B
Timer: 2 minutes — Discuss all options

Collaborative Task B

Your school wants to improve students' health and wellbeing. Here are some ideas and a question for you to discuss. First, talk to each other about how effective each idea would be. Then decide which two ideas the school should introduce.

Discuss these options
  • 🥗 A healthy eating café replacing the tuck shop
  • 🧘 A quiet room for relaxation and mindfulness
  • 🏃 Free fitness classes before and after school
  • 😴 A later start time for school mornings
  • 📱 A digital wellbeing workshop each term

Phase 2 (after timer): Decide together which TWO ideas would have the biggest impact.

PART 3 — Decision
Timer: 1 minute — Make your final decision

Make Your Decision

Now decide together: which two ideas should the school introduce? Justify your choices.

Remember

There is no right answer. The examiner is listening to HOW you discuss, not WHAT you decide.

PART 4 — Discussion

Part 4: Extended Discussion

The examiner asks further questions related to the Part 3 topic. Candidates discuss together and with the examiner.

Format

Duration: 4-5 minutes

Focus: Expressing and justifying opinions, agreeing/disagreeing, speculating about broader themes

Skills tested:

  • Discussing abstract and social issues
  • Expressing opinions with reasons and examples
  • Responding to and challenging partner's ideas
  • Speculating about the future
  • Using a range of vocabulary and structures

This is the highest-level part. Show you can discuss ideas, not just facts. Use phrases like "I think the main issue is...", "In my country...", "I suppose it depends on..."

PART 4 — Practice
Timer: 3 minutes — Discuss all questions

Extended Discussion

Discuss these questions together. Give your opinions and reasons. The timer starts automatically.

  1. Do you think young people today are healthier or less healthy than previous generations? Why?
  2. How important is it for schools to teach students about healthy living?
  3. Some people say that social media has a negative effect on young people's wellbeing. Do you agree?
  4. What do you think is the biggest challenge facing young people's mental health today?
  5. How could governments do more to support young people's health and wellbeing?
Top tip

Extend your answers with examples: "For example, in my school...", "I read that...", "My friend told me that..."

Assessment Criteria

Candidates are marked on four areas, each scored 0-5:

Grammar & Vocabulary

Range and accuracy of grammar. Variety and appropriacy of vocabulary. Can you use complex structures?

Discourse Management

Can you organise your ideas clearly? Use linking words? Keep going without long pauses?

Pronunciation

Clear and understandable? Correct stress and intonation? Individual sounds accurate?

Interactive Communication

Do you respond to your partner? Start and maintain conversation? Ask for opinions?

Useful Phrases

Comparing (Part 2)

  • Both photos show...
  • In the first photo... whereas in the second...
  • The main difference is that...
  • One similarity is...
  • This person looks... while that one seems...

Discussing (Parts 3 & 4)

  • I think... because...
  • That's a good point, but...
  • I see what you mean, however...
  • How about...? / What about...?
  • I agree with you. Also...

Speculating

  • I suppose it depends on...
  • It might be that...
  • I imagine most people would...
  • It's difficult to say, but...
  • Perhaps the reason is...

Buying Time

  • That's an interesting question...
  • Let me think about that...
  • Well, I'm not sure, but...
  • I haven't thought about that before, but...
  • Off the top of my head...
