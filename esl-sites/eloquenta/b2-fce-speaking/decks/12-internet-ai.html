The Internet & AI

Practice Part 1 interview, Part 2 photo comparison, Part 3 collaborative task, and extra homework.

Part 1 — Interview

Part 1 — Interview

Answer these questions. Spend about 20 seconds on each.

• How much time do you spend online every day?
I would say I spend about four or five hours online every day. That includes schoolwork, messaging friends, and watching videos.
• Do you think the internet has made life easier?
I think the internet has made many things easier, like communication and research. However, it can also be distracting and sometimes overwhelming.
• Have you ever used an AI tool?
Yes, I have used AI tools for help with homework and grammar checking. They are useful, but I make sure I understand the answers myself.
• What do you mainly use the internet for?
I mainly use the internet for school, social media, and entertainment. I also use it to learn new skills through tutorials.
• Do you worry about your online privacy?
Yes, I do worry about privacy. I try not to share too much personal information, but it is hard to know who can see your data.
Useful phrases:
I would say that...From my point of view...That's a tricky question because...On the one hand... on the other hand...I tend to...It depends on the situation.
Part 2 — Long Turn

Part 2 — Photo Comparison

Talk for 1 minute. Compare the photos and answer the question.

How might AI change the way students learn in the future?
Photo A:
A student studying alone with books and handwritten notes
Photo B:
A student using a tablet with an AI learning assistant
In photo A, a student is learning in a traditional way with books and notes, which helps develop independent thinking. In photo B, a student is using AI on a tablet, which can give instant feedback and personalised exercises. I think AI will change learning by making it more flexible, but it could also make students lazy if they rely on it too much. The best approach is probably a balance: students use AI for support but still do their own thinking and writing.
Part 3 — Discussion

Part 3 — Collaborative Task

Discuss with your partner for 2–3 minutes, then decide.

A school is deciding how students should use AI responsibly. Discuss the ideas and decide which two rules would be most important.
  • AI can only be used for checking grammar
  • Students must declare when they use AI
  • AI is banned for homework
  • Teachers must explain how AI works
  • AI can be used for research but not writing essays
A: I think students should have to declare when they use AI. That makes them honest and responsible.
B: That's a good point, but teachers also need to explain how AI works so students understand its limits.
A: I agree. Banning AI completely is unrealistic because students will use it anyway.
B: Yes. Allowing AI for research but not for writing essays seems fair. It helps students learn without doing the work for them.
A: So let's choose declaring AI use and limiting it to research.
B: Yes, I agree with you.
Part 4 — Extension

Part 4 — Follow-up Discussion

Answer these deeper questions.

• Is social media good for young people?
Social media can be good for connecting with friends, but it can also harm mental health. It depends on how much time you spend and what content you follow.
• Should AI replace teachers?
I do not think AI should replace teachers. It can help with marking and explanations, but teachers understand emotions and motivate students.
• How can people avoid misinformation online?
People should check information from multiple sources and avoid trusting headlines without reading the full article. Critical thinking is essential.
• What laws should exist for AI?
There should be laws about data privacy, transparent AI decisions, and clear rules about who is responsible when AI causes harm.
Vocabulary

Vocabulary & Phrases

artificial intelligence — computer systems that can perform tasks that normally need human intelligence

algorithm — a set of rules followed by a computer to solve problems

privacy — the right to keep your personal information secret

misinformation — false information that is spread, whether or not the person knows it is false

addicted — unable to stop using or doing something
