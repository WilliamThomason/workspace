Books & Reading

Practice Part 1 interview, Part 2 photo comparison, Part 3 collaborative task, and extra homework.

Part 1 — Interview

Part 1 — Interview

Answer these questions. Spend about 20 seconds on each.

• Do you enjoy reading?
I would say I enjoy reading, but I do not have as much time as I would like. I usually read before bed or during holidays.
• What kind of books do you prefer?
I prefer novels, especially thrillers and science fiction. I like books that keep me guessing until the end.
• How often do you read in English?
I try to read in English at least once a week. It helps me learn new vocabulary and see how sentences are structured.
• Do you prefer paper books or e-books?
I prefer paper books because I like the feeling of turning pages. However, e-books are much more convenient for travelling.
• Has a book ever changed the way you think?
Yes, a book about climate change made me more aware of environmental issues. It encouraged me to reduce my plastic use.
Useful phrases:
I would say that...From my point of view...That's a tricky question because...On the one hand... on the other hand...I tend to...It depends on the situation.
Part 2 — Long Turn

Part 2 — Photo Comparison

Talk for 1 minute. Compare the photos and answer the question.

Why do some young people prefer reading online articles to books?
Photo A:
A teenager reading a long novel in a quiet library
Photo B:
A teenager scrolling through short articles on a phone
In photo A, a student is reading a long book in a library, which takes concentration and time. In photo B, someone is looking at short articles on a phone, which is quicker and more interactive. I think young people prefer online articles because they are short and easy to access. Books require more patience. On the other hand, books usually offer deeper stories and better language development. I think both are useful, but for serious reading, books are still better.
Part 3 — Discussion

Part 3 — Collaborative Task

Discuss with your partner for 2–3 minutes, then decide.

A school wants to encourage more students to read. Discuss the ideas and decide which two would be most effective.
  • A school library with comfortable spaces
  • Book clubs during lunch
  • Recommended reading lists
  • Author visits
  • Reading challenges with prizes
A: I think book clubs are great because reading becomes social and students can discuss what they think.
B: That's a good point, but some students need a comfortable place to read first. A nice library space could make reading more appealing.
A: I agree. Author visits might be inspiring, but they only happen occasionally and might not reach everyone.
B: What about reading challenges? Prizes could motivate competitive students.
A: So let's choose book clubs and a comfortable library.
B: Yes, I agree with you.
Part 4 — Extension

Part 4 — Follow-up Discussion

Answer these deeper questions.

• Should reading be taught differently in schools?
I think reading should include more choice. If students can pick books they enjoy, they are more likely to read outside school.
• Are printed books going to disappear?
I do not think printed books will disappear completely. Many people still love physical books, even if e-books are popular.
• How does reading help language learning?
Reading helps you learn vocabulary in context and understand grammar naturally. It also improves your imagination and concentration.
• What makes a good story?
A good story needs interesting characters, a strong plot, and some emotion. If the reader cares about what happens, the story works.
Vocabulary

Vocabulary & Phrases

novel — a long written story about imaginary people and events

non-fiction — writing about real facts and events, not imaginary stories

plot — the series of events that form the main story

character — a person in a book, film, or play

recommend — to tell someone that something is good and worth using or doing
